They were figuring it out in real time, pulling from the only system they knew, the one their own parents handed them out of the Jim Crow South. Here's what that looked like in practice. If you grew up black in Gen X, you received two educations, one at school, one at home. And the one at home was more serious. The school education taught you reading and math. The home education taught you survival choreography. There were documented rules in black households from Virginia to Los Angeles, from Chicago to Atlanta that covered things no teacher ever put on a lesson plan. Never call a white adult by their first name, no matter how friendly they seem. Don't come across as too confident in white spaces. That reads as threatening. When you're in a white person's house, don't touch things. Speak when spoken to. Say yes ma'am and yes sir to people who may not respect you back. These rules weren't arbitrary. Your parents weren't controlling you for the sake of it. These rules were inherited survival codes from the Jim Crow era being translated in real time into a post civil rights America that hadn't fully changed as much as the laws suggested. A USC professor of electrical engineering, now a vice dean, shared how he grew up as part of the only black family in a rural Ohio town in the 1970s. His neighbors had explicitly racist signs on their lawns, used slurs in everyday conversation. His parents gave him the talk. They told him to always be polite toward white adults, to never address them by their first name, and not to come across so confidently that it could be perceived as uppidity. The talk, he said, was about being cautious, but not so cautious you become a coward. You probably had a version of that conversation in your specific house with your specific parent using your specific family's words for it. And you understood even as a child that following those rules wasn't about manners. It was about coming home safe. That is not a small thing to place inside a child. The scholar and civil rights thinker web deo wrote about this in 1903. He called it double consciousness. He described it as the sensation of always looking at yourself through the eyes of others, measuring your own soul against a world that looks back at you with something between contempt and pity. Living with what he called a tweness, two souls, two thoughts, two unreconciled strivings inside one body. The boys wrote that in 1903 and black Gen X children were living it in integrated classrooms in 1975. Because here's what integration actually meant on the ground. It didn't just mean sitting next to white students. It meant performing a version of yourself that you didn't have to perform at home. It meant your home self and your school self were two different operating systems running simultaneously and switching between them had to be seamless or there would be consequences. At home, you were Deshawn, Kesha, Marcus, a full human being in a community that knew your whole family that had your back that didn't require explanation. At school, you were an ambassador, possibly the only black child in your class under a microscope, carrying the invisible assignment of not giving them a reason, not confirming what they expected. And when you got home, nobody asked you how it felt because asking how it felt was not part of the vocabulary. Now, here's the part that separates Black Gen X from the broader Gen X experience. That story is real. But it's only half the story of black Gen X because black Gen X children were physically unsupervised and socially surveiled at the same time. It was a live active surveillance and accountability network made up of neighbors, church members, extended family, the woman at the corner store, the deacon down the block, the grandmother two doors over who had been in that neighborhood since before your parents were born. If Miss Johnson saw you doing something you shouldn't be doing, she didn't call your parents. She stopped you herself and then she called your parents. Every adult in the community had implicit authority over every child in the community. Not as strangers, as village members with a stake in how you turned out. They disciplined me without a second thought because they wanted to make sure I grew up with the skills to become a productive member of the community. That's not the latch key experience. That's something older, something before the nuclear family was the default. Something African in its roots, translated through generations of American survival into the specific form it took in a black neighborhood in 1972. The effect of growing up inside that village wasn't just protection. It was the formation of a specific kind of character. You understood that your behavior was never private. That what you did reflected on your family, that the community was watching and the community had standards and the community would hold you to them whether you liked it or not. Some people carry that as a burden, a hyper vigilance that never fully powers down. Some people carry it as a foundation, a deep sense of accountability to something larger than themselves. Most black Gen X adults carry both at the same time. Now, let's talk about what none of this prepared you for. Your parents gave you a curriculum for surviving in a world that might see you as a threat. They gave you tools for navigating white spaces with grace and precision. They handed you the villages collective standards for conduct and character. They taught you through the way they lived that strength meant not showing weakness, that you kept your business in the house, that you handled things. It's a consistent documented gap that shows up year after year. Some of that gap is access. Some of it is the real and documented shortage of culturally competent providers. But some of it, a significant portion, is the direct inheritance of how black Gen X was raised. You were taught that you don't need help. That needing help means something is wrong with you. That the people who went to therapy were the people who couldn't handle their business. That what happens in this house stays in this house. And we don't heir our business to strangers. You were not told that. But you absorbed it the same way you absorbed everything else in your household through observation, through imitation, through the unspoken rules that governed every room you ever sat in as a child. And so you became the person who handles everything. Who doesn't ask for help, who reads a room in 3 seconds and adjusts, who can be in a difficult conversation and not let a single thing show on your face. Who carried the weight of integration and village accountability and twess and the talk and all of it and kept moving. It doesn't mean you're broken. It means you were trained by people who loved you deeply in a world that required extraordinary measures.
